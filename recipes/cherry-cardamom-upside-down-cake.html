Cherry Cardamom Upside Down Cake

Description

A decadent spiced cake with cherries. This cake isn't very tall, but it's so rich that a thin layer adorned with cherries is all you need.

Ingredients

Cake Batter

  • 1/2 cup butter (1 stick)
  • 1/2 cup sugar*
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1 1/4 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground cardamom
  • 1 1/4 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup whole milk**

Topping*

  • 1 cup pitted fresh or frozen cherries
  • 2 tablespoons butter, melted (1/4 stick)
  • 1/4 cup brown sugar, packed

Directions

  1. Prepare an 8in x 8in square baking pan by buttering it and lining the bottom with parchment paper.
  2. In a small bowl, mix together flour, cardamom, baking powder, and salt. Set aside.
  3. In a separate mixing bowl, cream the one stick of butter with the sugar. Then beat in the egg and vanilla.
  4. Add the flour mixture, alternating with the milk in 3 additions of flour and 2 additions of milk.***See notes below.
  5. Preheat oven to 350 degrees Fahrenheit.
  6. In a microwave safe bowl, melt the 2 tablespoons of butter. Pour into the bottom of the pan. Add the brown sugar in a thin and even layer, followed by the cherries.
  7. Pour the batter over the butter,brown sugar, and cherries.
  8. Bake for 45-50 minutes or until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out clean.
  9. Remove from oven, and let rest for 10 minutes. Use a plate to flip over cake, and peel off parchment paper.
NOTES:
*The original recipe calls for 2 cups of cherries, 1/4 cup of butter (half stick), and 1/3 cup packed brown sugar in the topping. The original recipe also calls for 3/4 cup sugar in the batter. The result is a cake that is overly sweet and excessively buttery. **Skim milk may be used in lieu of whole milk. ***"Why do you have to alternate? And why do you begin with the dry and end with the dry?

It’s all about the butter. That whipped butter and sugar mixture cannot absorb much liquid. If you began with liquid, or worse added all the liquid, the butter will become saturated, the batter will separate, and the liquid will stay on top. At that point, adding dry ingredients will absorb that liquid but the resulting batter will be wrong and the cake will be heavy. You’ll have tasty concrete, not subtle cake.

So, follow those instructions and begin with the dry, alternate with the liquid, and carefully craft your batter. After each addition of the dry ingredients, mix only until most of it is incorporated. Some streaks of dry are fine. If you try to get every round of dry ingredients totally incorporated, you’ll begin activation of the gluten in the flour. Your cake will be tough, not light."

--from Perfect Cakes by Nick Malgieri by way of www.cookingbythebook.com
